Tuberculosis (TB) Diagnosis & Treatment

Coughing for more than two weeks? Unexplained weight loss, recurring fever or night sweats? These may indicate TB — a serious but completely treatable disease when diagnosed early and managed correctly.

Understanding tuberculosis

A persistent cough should never be ignored.

Have you been coughing for more than two weeks, or experiencing unexplained weight loss, recurring fever, night sweats or fatigue? These symptoms may indicate Tuberculosis (TB) — a serious but completely treatable infectious disease when diagnosed early and managed correctly.

Tuberculosis is an infectious disease caused by bacteria that most commonly affects the lungs, although it can involve other parts of the body as well. TB spreads through the air when an infected person coughs, sneezes or speaks. Despite being common, TB is often misunderstood — with timely diagnosis, proper medication and regular monitoring, the majority of patients can achieve complete recovery.

At Healthy Chest & Sleep Clinic, Dr. Vishal Sharma provides comprehensive evaluation, diagnosis, treatment and follow-up care for patients with Tuberculosis, helping them recover safely while preventing complications and disease spread.

Symptoms you should not ignore.

You should seek medical evaluation if you experience any of the following. Many patients initially mistake TB symptoms for a prolonged chest infection or seasonal illness, leading to delays in treatment.

Cough lasting over two weeks

A persistent, lingering cough

Blood in sputum

Coughing up blood-stained phlegm

Persistent fever

Fever that keeps returning

Night sweats

Sweating heavily during sleep

Unexplained weight loss

Losing weight without trying

Loss of appetite

Reduced desire to eat

Chest pain

Discomfort in the chest

Fatigue and weakness

Persistent tiredness

Breathlessness in some cases

Shortness of breath on exertion

Who is at higher risk?

Individuals who need early evaluation.

TB can affect anyone, but certain individuals may have a higher risk. Early evaluation is particularly important in these situations.

Close contact with a TB patient

Household or close exposure.

Weakened immunity

Reduced ability to fight infection.

Diabetic patients

Higher susceptibility to infection.

Smokers

Weakened lung defences.

Chronic lung diseases

Existing respiratory conditions.

Crowded living environments

Higher risk of transmission.

Immunosuppressive treatment

Patients on such therapy.

Our approach

How we diagnose & treat TB.

The goal is to establish an accurate diagnosis as early as possible, then build a personalised treatment plan and monitor recovery closely.

Detailed clinical assessment

A comprehensive review of symptoms, exposure history, medical background and risk factors.

Diagnostic evaluation

Chest X-ray, HRCT chest, sputum testing, CBNAAT/GeneXpert, blood investigations and oxygen assessment.

Drug sensitivity evaluation

Additional testing where needed to determine resistance and the most effective plan from the start.

Personalised treatment planning

Appropriate treatment planning with monitoring of medication response.

Managing side effects

Careful management to keep treatment on track and comfortable.

Ensuring treatment completion

Regular follow-up to ensure completion and prevent relapse.

Why completing treatment matters

Don't stop medication early.

Many patients begin feeling better within a few weeks, but stopping medication early can lead to serious problems. Completing the full course is one of the most important factors in complete recovery.
