Pleuroscopy / Thoracoscopy

When fluid repeatedly accumulates around the lungs or the cause of a pleural disease remains uncertain, pleuroscopy allows direct visualisation of the pleural cavity for accurate answers and effective treatment.

What is pleuroscopy

Advanced diagnosis & treatment for pleural diseases.

Pleuroscopy, also known as medical thoracoscopy, is an advanced minimally invasive procedure that allows direct visualisation of the pleural cavity — the space between the lungs and the chest wall — using a thin camera-equipped instrument called a thoracoscope.

Unlike CT scans and X-rays that provide images from outside the body, pleuroscopy allows direct visualisation of the pleura and enables targeted biopsies from abnormal areas, making it one of the most valuable procedures for diagnosing unexplained pleural diseases.

At Healthy Chest & Sleep Clinic, Dr. Vishal Sharma offers pleuroscopy / thoracoscopy for the diagnosis and management of complex pleural disorders without the need for major surgery in many cases.

When is it recommended?

Signs pleuroscopy may help.

Your pulmonologist may recommend pleuroscopy when routine investigations cannot determine the cause of a pleural problem.

Recurrent pleural effusion

Fluid repeatedly accumulates despite treatment

Unexplained pleural effusion

Cause unclear after fluid analysis and imaging

Suspected pleural tuberculosis

TB suspected but not confirmed by routine tests

Suspected pleural cancer

To obtain tissue for a definitive diagnosis

Pleural thickening

Abnormal thickening seen on CT scans

Persistent chest symptoms

Breathlessness, chest pain or unexplained findings

Diagnostic range

Conditions commonly diagnosed.

Pleuroscopy is frequently used in the diagnosis of a wide range of pleural diseases that require direct visualisation and biopsy.

Pleural tuberculosis

One of the most common causes of unexplained pleural effusion in India.

Pleural malignancy

Including metastatic cancers affecting the pleura.

Mesothelioma

A rare cancer involving the pleural lining.

Chronic pleural inflammation

Persistent inflammation requiring tissue diagnosis.

Recurrent pleural effusions

When the underlying cause remains uncertain.

Pleural thickening & nodules

Abnormal findings requiring biopsy and evaluation.

A clear advantage

Why pleuroscopy is superior to blind biopsy.

In the past, tissue samples were often obtained through blind biopsy techniques. These procedures had limitations because the doctor could not directly see the abnormal area.

Pleuroscopy allows direct visualisation, targeted biopsy, higher diagnostic accuracy, better tissue sampling and improved treatment planning — significantly improving the likelihood of obtaining a definitive diagnosis.

Step by step

How the procedure is performed.

Pleuroscopy follows a careful, structured process designed to ensure comfort, safety and an accurate diagnosis.

Detailed pre-procedure assessment

Symptoms, CT findings, X-rays, pleural fluid reports, medical and medication history are reviewed.

Patient preparation

The procedure is typically performed under sedation and local anaesthesia, prioritising comfort and safety.

Accessing the pleural space

A small opening is created in the chest wall to introduce the thoracoscope into the pleural cavity.

Direct visual assessment

The pleural cavity is examined for inflammation, nodules, thickening, infection and abnormal tissue.

Targeted tissue biopsy

Samples are obtained from abnormal areas for histopathology, TB testing and cancer diagnosis.

More than diagnosis

Therapeutic benefits of pleuroscopy.

Pleuroscopy is not only a diagnostic procedure. In selected patients, it can also be used therapeutically.

Pleural fluid drainage

Large pleural effusions causing breathlessness can be drained.

Pleurodesis

A procedure performed to prevent recurrent fluid accumulation in certain patients.

Symptom relief

Reducing fluid often leads to immediate improvement in breathing comfort.
