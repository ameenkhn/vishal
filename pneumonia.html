Pneumonia Treatment

A fever that won't settle. A cough that keeps getting worse. Breathlessness that wasn't there before. When a chest infection becomes serious, prompt diagnosis and treatment protect your lungs.

Understanding pneumonia

When a chest infection becomes serious.

Many people assume a fever that won't settle or a worsening cough is just a seasonal infection and wait for it to improve on its own. However, in some cases the infection can spread deeper into the lungs and develop into pneumonia — a potentially serious condition that requires prompt medical attention.

Pneumonia is an infection that affects the air sacs (alveoli) within the lungs. These normally fill with air during breathing, but in pneumonia they become inflamed and may fill with fluid or pus, making it harder for oxygen to move into the bloodstream. It can range from mild to life-threatening depending on the patient's age, overall health and the severity of the infection.

Who is at higher risk?

Individuals at greater risk.

While pneumonia can affect anyone, certain individuals are at greater risk of severe infection.

Older adults

Age-related decline in immunity increases vulnerability.

Smokers

Smoking damages the lungs' natural defence mechanisms.

COPD & asthma patients

Existing respiratory disease increases the risk of severe infection.

Diabetic patients

Diabetes can affect the body's ability to fight infection.

Weakened immunity

Cancer patients and transplant recipients are more susceptible.

Heart, kidney or liver disease

Underlying conditions can increase the severity of pneumonia.

Why pneumonia should not be ignored

Risks of untreated pneumonia.

Untreated or severe pneumonia may lead to serious complications. Prompt treatment helps minimise these risks and promotes faster recovery.

Respiratory failure

The lungs struggle to supply oxygen.

Pleural effusion

Fluid building up around the lungs.

Lung abscess

A pocket of infection in the lung.

Sepsis

A dangerous body-wide response to infection.

Hospitalisation

Severe cases may require admission.

Long-term lung complications

Potential lasting effects on the lungs.

Our approach

How we care for pneumonia.

Understanding the complete clinical picture helps guide effective treatment, from assessment and imaging through to recovery monitoring.

Comprehensive clinical assessment

Symptoms, medical history, existing conditions, medication and smoking history, and oxygen assessment.

Imaging evaluation

Chest X-ray, HRCT chest when indicated and follow-up imaging to monitor recovery.

Identifying the cause

Determining whether the infection is bacterial, viral, fungal or aspiration-related.

Personalised treatment planning

Tailored to severity, age, existing conditions, oxygen levels and risk factors.

Supportive care & oxygen support

Medications, supportive care, oxygen support and close monitoring when required.

Monitoring recovery

Ongoing follow-up of oxygen levels, persistent symptoms and radiological improvement.

Preventing future pneumonia

Reducing your future risk.

Several measures can help reduce the risk of future pneumonia and support healthier lungs.

Vaccination

Appropriate respiratory vaccinations can reduce the risk of certain forms of pneumonia.

Smoking cessation

Stopping smoking helps strengthen the lungs' natural defence mechanisms.

Managing chronic lung disease

Proper control of asthma, COPD and other conditions reduces infection risk.

Healthy lifestyle habits

Good nutrition, regular exercise and adequate sleep support overall immunity.
